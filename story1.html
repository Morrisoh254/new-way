The Testimony of

MCA Tricky{tricky sana}.

This is the story of Mca Tricky. — a testimony of redemption, restoration, and the light that overcomes darkness. Read prayerfully and be encouraged.

Francis Munyao, popularly known as MCA Tricky, was born in Kibwezi Constituency (Makindu) as the last born of three children. Life at home was tough—so difficult that he once shared on radio, "Nilipata home nikunoma." Yet despite these hardships, he never blamed his parents for the situation he found himself in. His journey of transformation began when people saw potential in him. Truly he was no ordinary child.As a young boy in a local school managed by Mr. Mbatia, the headteacher recognized something special in him. Francis attended school with almost nothing—"almost naked"—yet his headmaster saw he was number one in his class with a potential that defied his circumstances.Mr Mbatia connected MCA Tricky in another primary school-Kiamani Boarding School with better study force where his potential would reach its peak.

His academic excellence continued at Kiamani Boarding school, where he completed his KCPE exams and managed to achieve 412 marks. The Kiamani headteacher and his senior managed to "pull strings" and brought him to a school on Ngong Road near Karen — Maasai High School. It was here he learned a profound lesson he still quotes and believes today: "Mungu wangu hananga bedroom" — His God has no bedroom, meaning God is everywhere and accessible to all.

"Mungu wangu hananga bedroom"

Joining Form 1 at Maasai High marked his first time coming to Nairobi. As usual, he was number one in his class. However, in Form 2 his sponsorship was cut short, and going home to him seemed impossible. So he stayed in Nairobi, hanging around the streets. He connected with one of his cousins and almost lost himself—he even started using drugs(gum). His cousin's place became his escape route when Nairobi became too fierce. During this dark time, he made a simple prayer to God, asking for just a basic school—something that seemed like a basic need to many was his prayer request. Nairobi had been so unfair to this young man that he decided to return home after surviving months on the streets.

When his parents saw him, they thought he had quit school and started joking around. He recalls, "It was hard to convince them" otherwise. Luckily he managed to join Kiangine Mixed Secondary School. One day as he was around his busines he came across a school paper that had Masai High Schools secretarys phone number,immediately he called, got an apointment and set off to Nairobi. He took his results to the secretary, and upon reviewing them, the director of Maasai High happened to be around. He became the first student to be readmitted.

"Shida inaeza kuskuma kwa destiny yako"—Problems can push you toward your destiny."

The Breaking Point

2010. Francis Munyao (MCA-Tricky), 17 years old, became a statistic. He slept in River Road alleyways, wrapped in newspaper against the cold. He learned which kibandas threw away food last. He discovered that cyber cafés let you sit for hours if you pretended to wait for an "important email from America." The hunger was constant. The shame was worse.

One night, huddled under a shop veranda, an older street comedian looked at him and said: "Wewe, unajua utacheka hadi ukose usingizi, lakini hii maisha ya mtaa itakumaliza." ("You can laugh until you lose sleep, but this street life will finish you.")

Something woke up in Francis—may God have intervened. He didn't want to die unseen. He didn't want his story to end in an alley. So he made a deal with God: "Give me a platform. I won't waste it on small jokes. I'll tell our story—the story of the unseen." He walked back to Makueni. Not to quit. To begin.

Transformation

The First Show (2016) January 16, 2016. Carnivore Grounds. Churchill Show auditions. Francis—now calling himself "MCA Tricky"—took the stage in second-hand shoes and borrowed confidence. No credits. No name. Just a boy who knew what hunger tasted like.

His set? "How to Survive Nairobi Without Money." He demonstrated entering a matatu like you own it. Eating at a kibanda while complaining about "too much salt" to get extra meat. Sleeping in cyber cafés with fake "America emails."

The crowd roared. Then he dropped the line that broke the internet:

"Street boys don't beg. We negotiate with destiny."

A fan recorded it. Posted it. By morning, #MCATricky trended #1 in Kenya. By week's end: 2 million views.

Churchill called personally;

first show changed everything. The boy who begged for bread was now getting paid to be himself.

Before
After

The Fame (2016–2019) From that viral moment, MCA Tricky became unstoppable. Churchill Show regular—every weekend, national TV. Corporate MC gigs paying more than his mother's yearly income. Radio stations fighting to book him. Social media explosion—Kenyans shared his clips like gospel.

But here's what fans didn't see: while trending on stage, MCA Tricky was still grinding in class. JKUAT. Mechanical Engineering. 8 AM lectures after 2 AM shows. Assignments between sound checks. Exams during tour season.

"People thought I was just a comedian," he says. "They didn't know I had a deadline: graduation."

The Graduation (2019) December 2019. Jomo Kenyatta University. Francis Munyao walked across that stage in graduation gown—while holding his Churchill Show award. The photos broke the internet again. This time, the caption wrote itself: "From Street Boy to Graduate."

Class of 2019. BSc Mechanical Engineering. "I graduated not because I was special, but because I refused to choose between my gift and my degree. Comedy fed my spirit. Education secured my future."

Graduation Day

"The same hands that once begged for bread now held a degree. The same mouth that knew hunger now spoke on national radio. What God has transformed, no one can undo."

The same mouth that once begged for bread had spoken on radio. The same hands that slept on concrete now held a degree. The same boy who ran from poverty had run straight into his purpose.
